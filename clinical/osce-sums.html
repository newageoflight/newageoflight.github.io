2.1 Glossary

2.1.1 Lesions

Macule
Spot. A small non-palpable area of colour change <1.5cm (Ph1CSG: <1cm) diam
Patch
Larger spot. A large area of colour change with smooth surface
Plaque
Palpable path. Palpable flat lesion >0.5cm (Ph1CSG: >1cm) diam. May be elevated or thickened w/o being visibly raised above skin surface
Papule
Small lump. Small palpable lesion <0.5cm (Ph1CSG: <1cm) diam
Nodule
Lump. Large palpable lesions (Ph1CSG: >1cm)
Vesicle
Tiny blister. Small fluid-filled blister <0.5cm diam
Bulla
Blister. Large fluid-filled blister
Cyst
Fluid filled space
Pustule
Pus filled papule
Erosion
Epidermal loss. Superficial epidermal and papillary dermal loss
Ulceration
Dermal loss. Circumscribed destruction of reticular dermis and subcutis -> scarring
Telangiectasia, spider naevi
widely dilated BV in outer skin layer

3 BGDA

3.1 Lymphatics

For this you should review the position of all the lymph nodes in the body. But we need to know cervical, axillary and inguinal. Note that lymph node nomenclature tends to vary esp. for cervical chains.

  • Cervical
    1. Make sure neck is fully exposed, incl. supraclavicular fossa
    2. Observe for scars, swelling, asymmetry
    3. After asking about tenderness, begin palpation with submental nodes and move along lower jaw feeling for submandibular nodes.
    4. Feel for pre-auricular nodes and then move behind the ear to palpate posterior auricular (mastoid) and occipital nodes
    5. Progress down neck in posterior triangle (formed by clavicle, anterior border of trapezius and posterior border of SCM) to scalene node behind insertion of scalenus at clavicle
    6. Now move fingers into anterior triangle (formed by mandible, midline, SCM belly) to palpate for superior and deep anterior cervical chains of lymph nodes
      • Superficial nodes may be easily palpable as they overlie SCM, but deep nodes much harder to feel, even when enlarged, because situated deep to SCM and/or are more deeply located in neck, closely located to and invested around neurovascular bundles
    7. Palpate supraclavicular fossae and feel behind the clavicle while getting patient to shrug shoulders
  • Axillary (note: in front of lying/sitting patient. support arm in resting abducted position)
    1. Palpate behind pec major (anterior axillary fold) to apex axillae
    2. Palpate over vallum lateralis pectoris up to apex axillae
    3. Palpate against plica posterior axillae
    4. Palpate over 上臂中侧面
  • Inguinal (examined from in front of supine patient. region should be exposed taking care to cover genitalia)
    1. Palpate below line of inguinal ligament (from ASIS to symphysis pubis)
    2. Palpate along 股管上部 (extending below midinguinal pt in slightly medial direction)

4.1 Ob/gyn

  • Menstrual
    • Relevant dates of menarche, menopause
    • Cycle information (cycle length, regularity)
    • Features of flow (prolongation, heaviness, pain)
    • Associated symptoms (breast tenderness, back pain, psychological manifestations)
  • Obstetric
    • Pregnancy history
      • Estimated duration (最近经期, estimated dates based on ultrasound)
      • Development of symptoms during pregnancy incl. symptoms common to pregnancy
      • Past pregnancies, both full term and non (miscarriages/terminations)
        • Gravid vs. par and reasons for diff
        • Any medical conditions during past pregnancies (e.g. specifically asking about diabetes, BP)
        • Parturition procession - normal vaginal (induction/instrumentation?) vs. C-section (elective v. emergency)
        • Postnatal period e.g. coping/psych impact/postnatal depression,feeding
    • Pregnancy symptoms
    • Impact of pregnancy, concerns

4.2 ENT

  • Throat
    1. Examine with tongue resting in mouth (possibly see tonsillar beds w/o manipulation)
    2. Ask patient to protrude tongue and say "aaah" - use depressor if necessary to get better view
      • Make sure they don't gag!
  • Ear (examine the good ear first to avoid cross contamination)
    1. Prepare auroscope
      • Use clean speculum for each patient (or each ear if any infection/discharge in ext canal which could be transferred)
      • Check batteries
    2. Examine ear externally
    3. Gently apply traction to pinna
      • Note any pain - pathology indicator
      • In adults, generally pull the pinna up and back; in children the canal is more horizontal and tends to only need to be pulled back. In any case you can adjust direction of traction as needed to straighten the canal when you are inserting the auroscope.
    4. Place tip of speculum into ear, asking them to remain still
      • Hold the auroscope placing the hand in a position whereby if bumped or if the patient moves suddenly the tip will not be able to extend unintentionally forward into the canal (usually this means side of hand gently leaning on side of face).
    5. While looking through auroscope, advance slowly towards tympanic membrane, noting canal wall and then adjust angle of scope to visualise membrane
    6. After removing auroscope, remove speculum and put in sterilising facility or dispose of if appropriate

6.1 Abdominal (GIT/Renal)

  • Position and exposure
    • Ask patient if he/she is comfortable to lie flat
    • Lie patient flat with only head resting on pillow
    • Arms resting by side
    • Expose abdomen from lower chest to pubic symphysis
      • For women, ensure breast coverage
  • General observation
    • Generalised wasting (esp of muscle mass)
    • Pallor of periphery and conjunctiva
    • Jaundice of skin and sclera
  • Inspection of abdomen
    • Inspect from above and tangential to surface
    • Localised swelling
    • General distension
    • Dilated veins
    • Scars
  • Systematic palpation of abdomen (NB: if they have pain in an area, or tenderness is expected anywhere, begin palpation away from there)
    1. Begin by asking the patient if there is any tenderness and warm hands
    2. Lightly palpate the abdomen for tenderness, moving systematically through each quadrant of the abdomen
    3. Observe the patient's face rather than your hands to detect any evidence of discomfort
    4. Repeat this process for deep palpation for masses or enlarged organs
  • Palpation and percussion of liver (reliable sign, but not necessarily indicative of hepatomegaly - lower edge may be palpable normally, and also if ptosed, can be palpated)
    1. Begin by palpating in R lower quadrant moving vertically up towards the costal margin lateral to lateral margin of rectus muscle
    2. Ask patient to breathe deeply and adjust position of hand during expiration to be in place prior to next inspiration. As the patient inspires, allow the hand to lift over the descending liver edge. Do not apply too much pressure or this lifting movement will not be felt.
    3. If the liver is not palpated, move upward 1-2cm until your hand is palpating against the costal margin
    4. Percuss the lower border of the liver. Place the hand in a position so that your percussed finger is parallel to and below the expected liver border in the MCL, and moving upwards, percuss softly. The lower edge of the liver is thin and the difference between the edge and abdominal cavity is subtle and hence percussion needs to be soft. Confirm the border changes position with respiration. Note the lower border (avoid drawing marks on patient)
    5. Percuss upper border of liver. Begin in ics3 and percuss firmly downwards. The upper border should be readily audible. Confirm the border changes position with respiration. Note the upper border (avoid drawing marks on patient).
    6. Measure liver span in the MCL. The lower border is based on palpation or percussion. It is not necessary to use tapes or rulers as the measurement is not precise.
      • This shold normally be 8-12cm. Measurement by clinical techniques underestimates the span of the liver and has poor reproducibility. Hence a liver judged to be large clinically is probably large, whereas a liver judged to be small clinically might not be small
  • Palpation of spleen (to be palpable, it needs to be enlarged 2x - reliable sign of splenomegaly)
    1. Place left hand posterolaterally over left lower ribs
    2. With the right hand, begin palpating in medial side of the L lower quadrant, moving upwards and laterally towards the costal margin. The hand should be placed obliquely (pointing towards axilla) so as the descending spleen hits the fingertips
    3. Ask the patient to breathe deeply and adjust position of the hand during expiration to be in place prior to the next inspiration. As the patient inspires, allow the fingertips to lift over the descending spleen. Do not apply too much pressure or this lifting movement will not be felt.
    4. If the spleen is not palpable, ask the patient to roll towards the examiner. Ensure the patient's left arm is not stretched backwards as this will tense the abdominal muscles. If necessary, place the patient's left arm across the upper chest. Palpate below costal margin.
  • Palpation of kidneys (not normally palpable, need to be significantly enlarged to palpate form anterior abdo surface - deep, bimanual)
    1. After palpating the spleen in the right lateral position return the patient to the supine position.
    2. Position the right hand over the left loin with the fingertips in the left renal angle. Position the fingertips of the left hand below the left costal margin, lateral to the rectus muscle and overlying the position of the right hand
    3. As the patient inspires deeply, press fingertips towards each other to fell for lower pole of descending kidney
    4. Ballottment: if kidney is palpated, attempt to lift kidney anteriorly by pressing fingertips of right hand, lying posteriorly, in an upward direction. This will bring the kidney towards and impact on the left hand.
    5. Repeat this procedure, placing the left hand behind the right kidney and the right hand below the right costal margin
  • General percussion (for ascites, gas distension - with fluid present, there will be a horizontal level at which the percussion note changes when percussing over gas (resonant) to fluid (dull)
    1. Ensure patient is lying flat
    2. Percuss from midline, moving laterally to left flank. Place finger parallel to rectus muscle. Dullness should not be evident until reaching the lateral abdominal wall.
      • Absence of flank dullness is a reliable sign for excluding ascites and hence it is not necessary to test for shifting dullness if there is no flank dullness
      • In contrast, flank dullness alone is not sufficiently reliable to conclude the presence of ascites and shifting dullness should be tested
    3. If dullness is detected earlier, then test for shifting dullness. (this needn't be performed if no flank dullness)
    4. Level of dullness should be noted (simply done by leaving the finger at the point of dullness) and the patient asked to roll over towards the right resting in the right lateral position. Wait 30s to allow fluid settlement
    5. Percuss at original point of dullness: shifting dullness evident if it has become resonant in lateral position. Further percussion laterally will confirm shifting dullness. Further percussion medially will asses extent of shifting dullness.
  • Auscultation (bowel sounds and bruits)
    1. Listen with diaphragm for normal bowel sounds due to peristalsis - intermittent gurgling noises. May occur infrequently, need to listen for at least 30s before concluding that bowel sounds are absent. Can be heart at any point on abdomen - usu place steth in mid-abdo. Described as present or absent.
    2. Listen with bell for briuts. Place steth in midline of abdomen above umbilicus to listen for bruit from abdominal aorta

7 AEA

7.1 Musculoskeletal

  • General approach to examining limb joints
    • Principles
      • Observe during hx taking for any movement limitation, abnormal gait, evidence of pain
      • Ensure adequate exposure to inspect joint and adjacent structures and to allow unimpeded movement of joint
      • Enquire about pain and tenderness before palpating the joint and attempting to move the joint
      • Compare examination findings on one side with the other side (always examine the normal side/joint first)
    • Inspection (describe observable abnormalities - don't dx - remember the terms varus, valgus, subluxation, dislocation - no specific deformities like "swan neck" or "boutonniere" even though we were taught those :/ )
      • Joint position at rest
      • Joint swelling
      • Joint deformity
      • Overlying skin changes
      • Appearance of surrounding structures, esp. muscle wasting
    • Palpation: to note:
      • Normal bony and soft tissue landmakrs
      • Abnormal swelling; should be able to determine consistency of abnormal swelling - hard, boggy, fluid. Not expected to dx
      • Tenderness and/or warmth
    • Movement (RoM assessed by passive mvt; active mvt assessed if testing for muscle weakness, neurological abnormalities or during functional assessments)
      • Test for RoM in all dirs applicable to joint examined. Describe RoM in degrees.
        • Test flexion, extension in hinge joints and all mvts in ball+socket
        • RoM can be increased as well as decreased
      • Test for excessive movement around a joint in normal directinos to assess joint stability (knee)
      • Feel for crepitus during movement of joint
      • Assess function (e.g. hand) and gait if relevant
  • Examination of hand and wrist incl. functional assessments
    • Inspect hands and wrists, begin with dorsal surface and then palmar surface
    • Palpate and assess movement of wrists, MCPs and IP joints
    • Assess function of hands
  • Examination of knee including joint stability
  • MSAL (musculoskeletal screening exam)
